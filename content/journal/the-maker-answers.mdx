Somewhere between you and the person actually making your work, most firms install a layer: an account manager, a project lead, a client-services team. It's presented as a courtesy — a single point of contact, someone who "speaks your language." Here, the layer doesn't exist. When you write to this studio, **the person who answers is the person at the bench.**

The usual argument for the layer is protection: makers should be left to make, shielded from meetings. The quiet truth is that the layer mostly protects the firm — it lets seniors sell the work and juniors build it without the client noticing the swap, and it turns every question you ask into a game of telephone billed at two people's time.

> Every handoff is a translation, and every translation loses exactly the details that mattered.

Think about what actually travels through that layer. You explain a subtlety of how your business runs — why Thursdays are different, why that one customer type needs handling another way. The account manager writes it down as best they can. The maker reads the note. What reaches the work is a summary of a summary, and the first time you see the result, you're explaining it again. The cost of the layer isn't the salary. It's everything that didn't survive the trip.

Working directly with the maker has a different texture. Questions come back fast and specific, because the person asking needs the answer to build. Decisions get made in the conversation, not scheduled after it. And when something needs to change, you're talking to someone who can change it — not someone who'll "take it back to the team."

There's an honesty mechanism in this, too. A maker who has to face you builds differently from one who reports to a buffer. Your standards are in the room the whole time. **That's the real reason the layer isn't here: not efficiency — accountability, with your name on it.**
