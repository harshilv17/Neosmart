A rule we hold ourselves to when writing about work: **name the outcome, not the technology.** "A system that clears your inbox triage" — not "an AI-powered solution." It sounds like a style preference. It's actually a way of staying honest.

Technology names describe what the builder used. Outcome names describe what the client got. The first kind of sentence can be true while the work is useless — plenty of businesses own something genuinely powered by AI that has changed nothing about their week. The second kind of sentence can't hide. Either the inbox triage is gone or it isn't, and the person who owns the inbox knows which.

> A technology can be impressive. Only an outcome can be checked.

This matters more, not less, in a moment when the technology is genuinely remarkable. When a tool is powerful, "we used the tool" starts to feel like an achievement in itself, and it becomes easy to ship the demonstration instead of the result. The discipline of naming the outcome forces a harder question into every scope: what recurring work, specifically, stops existing when this is done? If the answer is vague, the project isn't ready — no matter how good the technology is.

It also changes what gets built. A project named after its technology is finished when the technology works. A project named after its outcome is finished when your Tuesday is different — when the screening that took your team a week takes an hour, and it's your people running it, in your stack, without us on the call. Those are different definitions of done, and they produce different work.

So the tools stay in the workshop, where they belong. What comes out of it is named after what it does for you — **because that's the only name you'd have any reason to check.**
