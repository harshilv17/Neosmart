“Done” means it runs in your hands.

By Aarav Mehta — who designs and builds the work this essay describes.

There's a moment at the end of most outsourced projects that nobody designs for: the day after the last invoice clears. The files have been delivered. The demo went well. Everyone said thank you. And then, a few weeks later, something small breaks — a form stops sending, a workflow needs one field changed — and the business that paid for the work discovers what it actually bought. Not a system. A dependency.

This failure isn't caused by bad developers or lazy designers. It's caused by how "done" gets defined. In most engagements, done means delivered — the work changed hands as a file, a repository, a login. Whether it keeps working in those hands was never anyone's job. The maker was paid for the artifact, not for the day after.

A deliverable answers the question "did we ship it?" Ownership answers a harder one: "can they run it without us?"

We define done the second way, and it changes how everything before it is built. A system meant to be run by your people can't be a clever black box — it has to be built in your stack, documented in your language, and simple enough at the edges that changing a field doesn't require calling anyone. A product meant to live on your infrastructure can't quietly depend on the builder's accounts. A brand meant to grow in your hands has to come with the files, the rules, and the reasoning — not a PDF and a goodbye.

Building this way is slower in places. It means writing things down that would be faster to just do. It means choosing boring, durable pieces over impressive fragile ones, and spending the last stretch of an engagement on handover — teaching, transferring, watching your team run it — when the conventional project would already be closed. We think that stretch is the point. It's the difference between work that ends and work that starts.

There's a quiet test for whether a studio believes any of this: ask what happens if you never call them again. If the honest answer is "things slowly stop working," you were sold a subscription wearing a project's clothes. The answer we build toward is simpler — everything keeps running, because it's yours.
